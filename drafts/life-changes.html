The only constant in life is change

It's been around a year since I pivoted from data to web development. By every conceivable metric, the past year has been a huge success.

Learning how to write production code at work gave me the confidence to work to take on side projects that were beyond my current skills and knowledge. Working on projects at the edge of my abilities has made me a better engineer. The only way I know how to git gud is by deliberate practice, so that's what I did.

I've been burning the candle at every possible end. Read books. Wrote blog posts. Contributed to open source. Got involved with Chicago Python (ChiPy). Worked on side projects. Started mentoring other developers. I did everything I could possibly think of to become a better programmer as fast humanly possible.

All this work culminated at PyCon 2018 where I presented a tutorial with a couple of my colleagues from ChiPy on Docker for Data Science. Putting together this tutorial was one of the most rewarding experiences of my life. It made me realize what I already knew...

I've also been finishing up my Masters in Medical Informatics over the past year. What can I say? It's been a busy year. As part of my degree requirements, I had to take a Leadership class which forced me to examine what kind of impact I want to have. I was not looking forward to this class, but it came around at the exact right time in my life. In our final essay, we had to write about our Personal Philosophy of Leadership and I wrote the following:

John Sonmez, Steve McConnell, and Uncle Bob have helped me distill what I want to do, which is to bring software engineer principles to the practice of data science in the field of healthcare technology. This means being recognized as a thought leader that speaks at conferences and teaches others about best practices that have been perfected through experience. I want to help other people become more effective at their jobs so we can innovate as an industry and solve the enormous number of problems that healthcare faces.

Looking back, I shoe-horned healthcare into this statement. Maybe I felt an obligation to work in healthcare because of all the time I had spent in the industry. Maybe it was the time and money it took to get my degree. I knew the industry, it's where I belonged... right?

When the healthcare startup I was working for pivoted back in March, I found myself looking for a new job. I applied to a bunch of Python jobs and I was hoping to snag one in healthcare. Fortunately all my hard work paid off and I ended up getting a job as a Backend Web Developer at an early-stage healthcare startup.

I thought I was good to go. The company was great. Both the CTO and Director of Engineering are encouraging mentors who wanted the best for their employees. It was a place I could build a career. It was a place where I could build a life.

I started my job right after PyCon and soon found myself looking forward to non-work hours. I was spending all my free time working on data-focused side projects, contributing to open source, and building out the Chicago Python community.

If I'm spending all my time outside of work doing things I actually enjoy, it's probably a sign that I should find a way to do those things for work. At PyCon, I had met a lot of Developer Advocate and knew that was what I wanted to do with my life.

One conversation that sticks out in particular is the chat I had with Matt Makai, former Developer Advocate at Twilio. He said that the best developer relation folks are the ones who have technical expertise cultivated through 4-5 years of industry experience. This is obviously not a hard and fast rule, but it's a recommendation based the technical aspects the job requires. He also pointed me to a resource he created aimed at advocates.

So I was back in Chicago working a job I didn't like very much. I had one foot out the door as I was still interviewing around. Had a phone interview for a Developer Advocate job at a data startup. It went really well. The company has an amazing product that I want to show the world how to use, but Matt's advice about industry experience stopped me from pursuing that opportunity any further.

I needed to take some time and become an expert. It meant that I needed to get out of my comfort zone and focus more on the job that will get me to where I want to be versus focusing on working in a specific industry.

I've also come to realize that just because I'm not working in healthcare doesn't mean I'm not using my degree. I’ve learned how to read and consume media with a critical mind. I now have a solid grasp of the written word and have started to write on my blog fairly regularly. And most importantly, I’ve picked up techniques that help me get things done. Be it reading an article from a medical journal I can barely comprehend or completing a project I know nothing about. The fact I had to get it done taught me how to improvise and figure it out.

After all of that... I'm happy to announce I am joining Nielsen as a Data Engineer where I will be building data pipelines in Airflow and helping data scientists write better, more performant Python.

I started in early October and I could not be happier. For the first time in a long time, I'm doing something I like.
